Industry Trends
Home Sales Sluggish
US home sale activity has remained muted in recent years, with only a slight pickup heading into 2025. According to the National Association of Realtors (NAR), existing-home sales in late 2025 rose to an annual pace of about 4.1 million, a modest gain from a year earlier. NAR also reported that the national median existing-home price reached roughly $415,200, marking the 28th consecutive month of year-over-year price increases. Inventory remains constrained: NAR estimates about 1.5 million homes on the market , or roughly a 4.4 month supply - still short of a balanced level. New single-family home activity has been similarly uneven, with the US Department of Housing and Urban Development noting that early-2025 sales hovered around a 657,000 annual rate. Overall, the market shows signs of gradual stabilization, but tight supply and elevated costs continue to limit a fuller recovery.

Home Prices Rise
US home prices cooled in recent years, but the market has since shown signs of stabilization and modest price growth. In 2023, the median sale price dropped to about $425,150, a notable pullback from the roughly $457,475 median in 2022. According to the National Association of Realtors, by late 2025 the median existing-home sale price stood at around $415,200, continuing a streak of year-over-year price increases. Data from the Federal Housing Finance Agency indicate that national house prices rose roughly 2.9% between the second quarter of 2024 and the second quarter of 2025. Though growth has slowed compared with the rapid run-up of the early 2020s, home prices remain elevated, contributing to continuing affordability challenges, especially for first-time buyers whose incomes have not kept pace with home-price growth.

Seesawing Interest Rates
Mortgage interest rates have remained elevated in 2023-2025 after rising sharply in prior years. The average 30-year fixed mortgage rate ended 2023 at approximately 6.6%, up from 6.1% at the end of 2022, and remained high throughout 2024, averaging about 6.8% for the year. In 2025, rates eased to roughly 5.7%, providing some relief to prospective buyers and helping support a modest rebound in home sales. Although still above the historically low levels seen earlier in the decade, these lower rates have improved affordability, particularly for younger generations such as Gen Z and Millennials, as well as first-time homebuyers who have faced challenges entering the market amid rising home prices and limited inventory. Overall, the shift in rates from 2024 to 2025 has had a meaningful impact on buyer behavior, slightly expanding the pool of households able to finance a home purchase.

Refinancing Activity Hampers Mortgage Originations
The value of mortgage originations continues to be highly volatile, largely driven by shifts in refinancing activity. According to the Mortgage Bankers Association, total originations for one- to four-family residences fell sharply in 2022 and 2023 following record highs in 2020 and 2021, with a decline of about 50% in 2022 and 27% in 2023. Refinancing activity, which had surged during low-rate periods, dropped 74% in 2022 and another 53% in 2023, reflecting the impact of higher mortgage rates on borrower demand. Purchase-originated mortgages were more stable, increasing modestly in 2022 but declining about 16% in 2023. By 2025, refinancing has begun a gradual recovery as mortgage rates eased from their 2024 highs, though the refinancing share of total originations remains well below the peaks seen in previous low-rate periods, highlighting the continuing sensitivity of the market to interest-rate movements.

Investors Buying More Homes
Investors are buying a larger share of single-family homes than normal in 2025, taking advantage of excess inventory amid high interest rates and tariffs, according to a report by property analytics company Cotality. In January 2025 investors bought a record high 32% of the available houses in the US. That fell to 29% by July, but it illustrates investors can get better deals than an individual home buyer. Cotality showed small investors - those with 10 or less properties - made up 14% of purchases, the highest share. Medium-sized investors (fewer than 100 properties) made up 10%, while large investors made up 5%. Smaller investors can be more flexible and don’t necessarily have to earn as much profit as a large investor would. Smaller investors are also taking advantage of “must sell” situations and higher interest rates to lure in more renters and earn ongoing income.
